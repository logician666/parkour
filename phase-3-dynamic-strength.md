# Phase 3 — Dynamic Strength `Weeks 5–6`

> **Goal:** Develop strength through full range of motion under load. Begin training explosive hip extension and push patterns.
> **Unlock criteria:** Phase 2 complete. 15 push-ups in one set. Wall sit 60s.
> **Progression note:** Week 6 repeats the same sessions as Week 5. Log your Week 5 numbers and beat them in Week 6.

## Session 1 — Lower Body Dynamic

**Warm-up:** 5 min — hip circles, hip hinges, lateral lunges, ankle circles

---

### Wall-Assisted Split Squat
**Sets · Reps · Rest:** 4 × 10 each leg · 60s rest
**Log type:** Reps

> **Why:** The single most transferable parkour strength exercise. Every vault push-off, precision landing, and take-off is a single-leg explosive event. This builds the quad, glute, and hip flexor strength to produce and absorb that force.

**How:** Stand facing away from the wall, about 60cm from it. Reach one foot back and press the top of that foot flat against the wall at shin height. Front foot flat on floor, directly under your knee. Lower your back knee toward the floor, torso upright. Push through the front heel to return. Complete all reps on one side before switching. Adjust distance from wall until comfortable.

**Cues:**
- Front shin stays roughly vertical — if knee shoots forward, move front foot further forward
- Torso upright — resist the lean
- Drive through the heel of the front foot, not the toes
- Start weaker leg first

*Search: "wall assisted split squat" for video reference.*

---

### Single-Leg Squat Jump
**Sets · Reps · Rest:** 4 × 6 each leg · 90s rest
**Log type:** Reps

> **Why:** Trains single-leg explosive push-off — the hip extension and knee drive that powers every vault and jump. Unilateral loading forces each leg to produce force independently, exposing and correcting the asymmetries that bilateral jumps mask.

**How:** Stand on one foot, knee slightly bent. Drive that knee and hip explosively, jumping as high as possible off one leg. The free leg swings forward and up to add momentum. Land softly on both feet. Complete all reps on one side before switching. Use a wall at fingertip reach for balance if needed.

**Cues:**
- Drive the hip fully — do not just push off the ankle
- Free leg knee drive adds significant height — use it
- Land on both feet, absorb softly — do not land stiff
- Log which leg is weaker — address it with an extra set

*Search: "single leg squat jump" for video reference.*

---

### Nordic Curl — Reduced Assistance
**Sets · Reps · Rest:** 3 × 6–8 reps · 90s rest
**Log type:** Reps (note how far you lower before using hands)

> **Why:** Progress from Phase 2's assisted version. Hamstring strength in full hip extension is the primary driver of explosive jump height and the primary preventer of hamstring tears.

**How:** Same setup as Phase 2 — kneel, feet hooked. This time, aim to lower at least 60% of the way to the floor before using hands. Push back with minimal hand assistance. Track your depth each session.

**Cues:**
- Body stays in one plane — knee to head
- Measure progress by how far you descend without hands
- If you crash at the bottom — the hands catch you. That is fine. Lower slower next time.
- The goal over these 2 weeks: full lowering, partial push with hands

*Search: "Nordic curl progression" for video reference.*

---

### Jump Squat to Stick
**Sets · Reps · Rest:** 4 × 6 reps · 90s rest
**Log type:** Quality (1–5 — rate your landing control)

> **Why:** The stick landing is the fundamental skill of parkour — absorbing force precisely and completely. This drill trains the nervous system to decelerate rapidly and maintain balance under impact, which cannot be developed through slow movements alone.

**How:** Perform a squat jump as in Phase 2. On landing, absorb into a quarter squat — then HOLD completely still for 3 full seconds. Do not adjust your feet, do not wobble. The held position is the training stimulus. If you wobble significantly, rate yourself 1–2. Steady hold = 4–5.

**Cues:**
- Stick means STOP — not slow down
- Arms can assist landing balance — extend forward as you land
- Look at a fixed point on the wall to stabilise balance
- Log your quality rating each session — it should climb

*Search: "precision landing training parkour" for video reference.*

**Cool-down:** 5 min — pigeon pose each side, kneeling hip flexor stretch, standing quad stretch

## Session 2 — Upper Body Dynamic

**Warm-up:** 5 min — wrist circles, bear crawl, arm swings, passive hang 20s

---

### Pike Push-Up
**Sets · Reps · Rest:** 4 × 8–12 reps · 60s rest
**Log type:** Reps

> **Why:** The pike push-up trains vertical pushing strength — the shoulder pattern used in wall runs and arm placements on high obstacles. It is the bodyweight precursor to the handstand push-up.

**How:** Start in a downward-dog position — hands shoulder-width on floor, hips high, body forms an inverted V. Bend elbows to lower the top of your head toward the floor between your hands. Push back up to the inverted V. The movement is entirely in the shoulder — not the elbow like a regular push-up.

**Cues:**
- Hips stay high throughout — don't let them drop
- Head tracks between hands — not in front of them
- Elbows flare slightly outward
- Full extension at top — push the floor away

*Search: "pike push-up tutorial" for video reference.*

---

### Door Frame Row
**Sets · Reps · Rest:** 4 × 10–12 reps · 60s rest
**Log type:** Reps

> **Why:** Pulling strength is the neglected half of parkour upper body — every ledge pull-up, climb-up, and hanging transition demands it. This uses the door frame already in your equipment list as the anchor for a horizontal pulling pattern.

**How:** Stand in your door frame. Grip both sides of the frame at chest height. Walk feet forward until your body leans back at roughly 45°, arms extended. Pull your chest to the frame by bending elbows, keeping body straight. Lower with control. The further your feet walk forward, the more horizontal your body and the harder the pull. Begin with feet close, progress forward over weeks.

**Cues:**
- Body forms a straight plank — hips don't sag
- Pull elbows back past your torso — not just toward the frame
- Squeeze shoulder blades together at the top
- Feet closer = easier. Feet further = harder.

*Search: "door frame row bodyweight" for video reference.*

---

### Diamond Push-Up
**Sets · Reps · Rest:** 3 × 8–12 reps · 60s rest
**Log type:** Reps

> **Why:** Tricep and anterior shoulder strength for the push phase of a vault — the moment when arms push off the obstacle surface to propel the body over. The narrow hand position isolates the triceps more directly than a standard push-up.

**How:** Start in push-up position. Place hands close together under your chest, thumbs and index fingers touching to form a diamond shape. Lower your chest toward your hands, keeping elbows tracking back along the body. Push back up to full extension. If too difficult, begin with knees on the floor, maintaining a straight hip-to-head line.

**Cues:**
- Elbows track back along the ribs — not flaring outward
- Chest descends to hands — not face to floor
- Full elbow extension at the top
- Core rigid throughout — no hip piking

*Search: "diamond push-up tutorial" for video reference.*

---

### Archer Push-Up
**Sets · Reps · Rest:** 3 × 6 each side · 90s rest
**Log type:** Reps

> **Why:** Develops unilateral pushing strength and shoulder stability — the specific pattern of weight-shifting and single-arm loading that occurs during vaults and complex arm placements.

**How:** Start in a wide push-up position — hands much wider than normal. Lower your chest toward one hand, allowing that elbow to bend while the other arm stays extended (nearly straight). Push back to centre. Lower to the other side. That is 1 rep per side.

**Cues:**
- The straight arm remains active — push the floor away with it
- Keep hips level throughout
- Move slow enough to feel the unilateral load
- Work toward arms fully extended on the non-working side

*Search: "archer push-up tutorial" for video reference.*

**Cool-down:** 5 min — doorway chest stretch, cross-body shoulder stretch, wrist flexor and extensor stretch

## Session 3 — Full Body Dynamic

**Warm-up:** 5 min — bear crawl, lateral lunges, arm circles, squat to stand

---

### Burpee (controlled)
**Sets · Reps · Rest:** 4 × 8 reps · 90s rest
**Log type:** Reps

> **Why:** The burpee is the full movement cycle of parkour distilled — a rapid transition from standing to ground and back. It trains the body to absorb and redirect force across planes quickly while maintaining body control.

**How:** Stand. Drop hands to floor, jump or step feet back to plank. Lower chest to floor. Push up. Jump or step feet forward to squat. Jump up, arms overhead. Land soft. That is 1 rep. Prioritise control over speed — this is not a conditioning race.

**Cues:**
- Land soft every rep — the floor contact is always a training signal
- Maintain plank position when in the down phase — no hip sag
- Breathe rhythmically — exhale on the push, inhale on the jump
- Log your form quality, not just the count

*Search: "burpee proper form" for video reference.*

---

### Explosive Bear Crawl
**Sets · Reps · Rest:** 4 × 6 explosive steps each direction · 90s rest
**Log type:** Quality (1–5)

> **Why:** Phase 1 bear crawl built the pattern. Now the goal is explosive, reactive quadrupedal movement — the basis of parkour ground movement, underbar entries, and rapid directional changes at low height.

**How:** Same starting position as Phase 1. Instead of slow deliberate steps, move as fast as possible for 6 steps forward, then 6 steps back. Between sets, assess: did hips stay level? Did you maintain coordination? Quality drops as speed increases — track where your form breaks.

**Cues:**
- Speed is the load — form is the metric
- Hips level even at high speed
- Breathe — don't hold breath under exertion
- Rate quality 1–5: 5 = fast and perfectly coordinated

*Search: "explosive bear crawl parkour" for video reference.*

---

### Tuck Jump
**Sets · Reps · Rest:** 4 × 6 reps · 90s rest
**Log type:** Reps

> **Why:** Trains hip flexion speed and body compression under ballistic load — the body position required for gap jumps, precision jumps over obstacles, and the tuck in vaulting.

**How:** Stand feet shoulder-width. Jump maximally and bring both knees to your chest simultaneously at the peak. Arms pull knees up. Extend legs and land softly in a quarter squat. The compression is the training — not the jump height.

**Cues:**
- Pull knees UP, not body DOWN — the distinction matters for the movement pattern
- Land soft every rep — use the arms to help absorb
- Do not rush the landing extension — control the knee drive and the land separately
- Quality before height: clean tuck > high sloppy jump

*Search: "tuck jump exercise" for video reference.*

---

### Dead Hang — Active to Passive Cycling
**Sets · Reps · Rest:** 3 × 30s · 60s rest
**Log type:** Time (seconds)

> **Why:** Progressive grip and shoulder endurance for hanging transitions. This cycling between active (scapulae depressed) and passive (relaxed) positions builds the connective tissue resilience required for ledge hangs and bar work.

**How:** Hang from door frame. Spend 10 seconds in active hang (shoulders pulled down), then 10 seconds fully passive (let shoulders rise to ears), then 10 seconds active again. That is one 30s set. The transition is the training — the tendons load and unload under body weight.

**Cues:**
- Passive means truly passive — don't resist the shoulder elevation
- Active means fully depressed — pull shoulders away from ears deliberately
- Grip should be firm but not white-knuckled
- Gradually increase time as grip endurance improves

*Search: "active vs passive hang" for video reference.*

**Cool-down:** 5 min — full spinal twist, pigeon pose, wrist prayer stretch, child's pose
