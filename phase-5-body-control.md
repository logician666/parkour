# Phase 5 — Body Control `Weeks 9–12`

> **Goal:** Balance, precision movement, quadrupedal flow, and spatial awareness — the artistry layer of parkour.
> **Unlock criteria:** Phase 4 complete. Reactive circuit quality stable across rounds. Silent depth drop landing.
> **Structure:** 4 themed weeks, 3 sessions per week. Each week builds a distinct quality. Week 12 integrates all four.

## Week 9 — Balance Foundation

### Session 1 — Static Balance

**Warm-up:** 5 min — ankle circles, single-leg calf raises ×15 each, hip hinges

---

#### Single-Leg Stance Progression
**Sets · Reps · Rest:** 4 × 60s each leg · 30s rest
**Log type:** Time held without touch-down (seconds)

> **Why:** Static single-leg balance is the baseline from which all parkour movement stability derives. Balance deficits invisible in bilateral movement become landing failures in parkour. This makes them visible and trains them away.

**How:** Stand on one foot. Progress through difficulty within the 60s: eyes open → eyes closed → eyes open with slow head turns. Count only continuous time without the free foot touching down. Log the longest unbroken hold per leg.

**Cues:**
- Micro-movements are expected — the foot and ankle are continuously correcting. That is correct.
- Focus on a fixed point (eyes open) or your body's internal signals (eyes closed)
- Arms out to sides for balance — gradually bring them to chest as ability improves
- Note which leg is weaker — address it specifically

---

#### Single-Leg Hip Hinge (balance emphasis)
**Sets · Reps · Rest:** 3 × 8 each leg · 45s rest
**Log type:** Reps

> **Why:** Balance under a moving load — the relevant scenario is a landing, where the body must remain stable while the centre of mass shifts forward. This trains exactly that pattern.

**How:** Stand on one leg, knee slightly bent. Hinge forward from the hip, extending the free leg back for counterbalance. Lower torso until parallel to floor (or as far as balance allows). Return upright. This is 1 rep. Use a wall at fingertip distance for emergency balance only.

**Cues:**
- Hinging leg stays slightly bent — never locked
- Free leg and torso form one line — don't let free leg drop
- If you touch the wall every rep, you are not training balance — move further away
- Track whether you can complete 8 without wall contact

---

#### Balance Board Simulation (folded mat or folded clothing)
**Sets · Reps · Rest:** 3 × 45s each leg · 30s rest
**Log type:** Time (seconds unbroken)

> **Why:** Unstable surface training develops the proprioceptive sensitivity of the ankle — the joint most critical to landing safety in parkour. A folded mat or tightly rolled clothing creates sufficient instability without any additional equipment.

**How:** Fold your mat (already in your equipment list) to create a firm but unstable surface, or roll a thick jacket tightly. Stand on one foot on the folded surface. Maintain balance. Progress: still → small arm movements → slow controlled torso rotation → eyes closed.

**Cues:**
- The ankle is doing most of the work — feel it
- Do not grip with the toes — let the ankle correct, not the foot
- The wobble is the training — do not try to eliminate it, try to manage it

**Cool-down:** 5 min — seated ankle circles, standing calf stretch, supine single-leg stretch

---

### Session 2 — Dynamic Balance

**Warm-up:** 5 min — single-leg stance 30s each, lateral lunges, leg swings

---

#### Walking Lunge — Eyes Closed Finish
**Sets · Reps · Rest:** 3 × 10 steps · 60s rest
**Log type:** Quality (1–5)

> **Why:** Combines dynamic balance with proprioceptive demand. Closing the eyes on the landing position removes visual stabilisation and forces the nervous system to rely on proprioception — the same system that must operate when landing at speed with visual attention on the environment, not the feet.

**How:** Perform walking lunges across your space. On each landing, close your eyes for 2 seconds before opening and stepping into the next rep.

**Cues:**
- The eyes-closed hold is the training stimulus — don't skip it
- Quality 5 = no wobble during eyes-closed phase
- If quality is consistently 1–2, reduce eyes-closed hold to 1 second and build up

---

#### Pistol Squat Progression
**Sets · Reps · Rest:** 4 × 5 each leg · 90s rest
**Log type:** Level achieved (1–4, see below)

> **Why:** The pistol squat is the full single-leg squat — maximum single-leg strength, balance, and hip mobility combined. In parkour it corresponds to absorbing a deep, single-leg landing from significant height. Progress through levels based on current ability.

**Levels:**
1. Assisted — hold door frame or wall, lower as deep as possible
2. Box pistol — lower onto a chair or low surface, stand up on one leg
3. Counter-weighted — hold arms or small weight out front for balance, full range
4. Unassisted — no support, full depth, controlled

**How:** Choose your level. Complete 5 controlled reps per leg. If Level 1: hold the support lightly, gradually use it less. If Level 4: arms across chest for maximum difficulty.

**Cues:**
- The free leg extends forward — do not let it touch the floor
- Knee tracks over the foot — no valgus collapse
- Lower is better only if control is maintained
- Log your level honestly — progress is the story, not the level

---

#### Lateral Step-Over (balance beam simulation)
**Sets · Reps · Rest:** 3 × 10 steps each direction · 45s rest
**Log type:** Quality (1–5)

> **Why:** Trains lateral weight transfer with balance precision — the skill used when traversing narrow obstacles and walls.

**How:** Use a line of tape on the floor as a balance beam. Walk laterally, stepping over an imaginary obstacle with each step (lift knee high, step wide, place precisely). Arms out to sides. Keep feet on the line.

**Cues:**
- Deliberate foot placement — precision over speed
- High knee on each step — simulate obstacle clearance
- Gradually narrow the "beam" as balance improves

**Cool-down:** 5 min — pigeon pose, standing hip flexor stretch, ankle circles

---

### Session 3 — Balance Under Load

**Warm-up:** 5 min — full joint circuit (wrist, ankle, hip, shoulder)

Repeat Session 1 and Session 2 key exercises in mini-circuit format. 1 set each, reduced load. This is a consolidation session — no new exercises.

**Focus:** identify your weaker leg and weaker balance direction. Spend any remaining time with additional single-leg work on that side.

**Log:** Note your weaker leg and the specific deficit (static hold, dynamic, pistol level). This informs Week 12 personalised work.

## Week 10 — Precision Movement

> **Precision** in parkour = the ability to land exactly where you intend, absorb completely, and remain still. This week trains the intentional placement of force in space.

### Session 1 — Precision Landing Drills

**Warm-up:** 8 min — squat jumps ×5, lateral bounds ×4 each, single-leg balance 30s each

---

#### Precision Jump to Tape Mark
**Sets · Reps · Rest:** 5 × 5 jumps · 90s rest
**Log type:** Accuracy (cm off target) + stick quality (1–5)

> **Why:** Jumps in parkour always have a target. Training without targets builds power but not precision. This drill introduces intentional targeting — you must both reach the mark and stop completely.

**How:** Place tape marks at varying distances (start at 80% of your broad jump maximum — a challenging but achievable target). Jump and land with both feet simultaneously on the mark. Stick the landing — hold 3s. Measure distance from your feet to the target mark.

**Cues:**
- Visualise the landing before every jump — see your feet on the mark
- Adjust take-off power based on previous rep's overshoot or undershoot
- The stick is mandatory — moving feet after landing = no score
- Log: distance off target (cm) and stick quality (1–5)

---

#### Foot Placement Walk (precision)
**Sets · Reps · Rest:** 4 × traverse your space × 2 · 45s rest
**Log type:** Quality (1–5 — deliberateness of each step)

> **Why:** Trains intentional foot placement — where you put your foot and how your weight arrives. The unthinking step pattern of normal walking is the enemy of precision movement in an environment where surface quality is unknown.

**How:** Walk slowly across your space, placing each foot with full deliberateness — choose the exact spot, then place the foot there. Lift the foot completely, pause momentarily, then lower it with control. Extreme slow motion. This feels ridiculous. Do it anyway.

**Cues:**
- Every step is chosen — no automatic stepping
- Feel the foot making contact: ball first, then distribute weight
- This trains the proprioceptive habit of knowing where your foot is before trusting it
- Progress: floor → walking on a line of tape with precision

---

#### Single-Leg Landing to Direction Change
**Sets · Reps · Rest:** 4 × 6 reps · 90s rest
**Log type:** Quality (1–5)

> **Why:** In urban parkour, you rarely land and stop — you land and continue moving. This drill trains the connection between landing absorption and immediate directional change without losing balance or control.

**How:** Jump forward and land on one foot. Hold for 1 second. Then step or push off immediately in a different direction (90°). The hold is non-negotiable — no stumble-stepping through it.

**Cues:**
- The 1-second hold is the reset — it proves you have control before committing to the next movement
- The direction change can be: forward to lateral, lateral to backward, etc.
- Quality 5 = clean land, clean hold, clean transition with no adjustment steps

**Cool-down:** 5 min — hip flexor series, quad stretch, full ankle sequence

---

### Session 2 — Hand Precision

**Warm-up:** 5 min — wrist circles, bear crawl, passive hang 20s, explosive push-up ×4

---

#### Precision Hand Placement Push-Up
**Sets · Reps · Rest:** 4 × 8 reps · 60s rest
**Log type:** Quality (1–5)

> **Why:** Vault hand placements must be precise — the wrong hand position on an obstacle means a failed or dangerous vault. This drill trains the hand to go exactly where it is directed.

**How:** Place two small tape marks on the floor, shoulder-width. These are your hand targets. Perform push-ups, placing your hands precisely on the marks with every rep. Lift your hands completely off the floor between reps and replace them on the marks deliberately.

**Cues:**
- Lift hands fully off the floor at the top of each rep
- Replace with intention — not a fall back into position
- If accuracy is low, slow down. Speed comes after precision.
- Vary mark positions each week to prevent anchoring

---

#### Arm Balance Hold (crow pose / frog stand)
**Sets · Reps · Rest:** 4 × hold max · 2 min rest
**Log type:** Time (seconds)

> **Why:** Develops wrist strength, shoulder proprioception, and body tension in an inverted arm support position — the strength base for vaulting movements that require momentary full weight on arms.

**How:** Squat down, hands flat on floor shoulder-width. Place knees on the backs of your upper arms (near the armpits). Lean forward gradually, shifting weight onto hands. Lift one foot, then try both feet off the floor. Hold. Begin with feet barely off the floor. Progress to a held position.

**Cues:**
- Lean forward more than feels natural — the weight must shift over the hands
- Core tight — the hips should not sag
- Look forward, not down
- Come down the way you went up — controlled

*Search: "crow pose / frog stand tutorial" for video reference.*

---

#### Quadruped Precision (gecko crawl)
**Sets · Reps · Rest:** 4 × traverse and return · 60s rest
**Log type:** Quality (1–5)

> **Why:** Gecko crawl trains the same-side limb coordination used in fast, precise ground movement — and forces extreme hip mobility and body awareness close to the ground, which is where some of the most technically demanding parkour movement occurs.

**How:** From all fours, move the right hand and right foot forward simultaneously (same-side, unlike the bear crawl). Keep hips low and weight distributed. This coordination pattern will feel wrong initially — that is the training.

**Cues:**
- Same-side is the rule — right hand, right foot move together
- Keep the movement slow enough to maintain coordination
- As coordination improves, gradually increase speed
- Alternate with bear crawl sets to feel the contrast in coordination patterns

**Cool-down:** 5 min — wrist decompression series, child's pose, spinal twist

---

### Session 3 — Precision Integration

Combine precision landing drills (Session 1, Exercise 1) with hand placement push-ups (Session 2, Exercise 1) and gecko crawl (Session 2, Exercise 3) in a circuit:

- Precision jump × 3
- Hand placement push-ups × 6
- Gecko crawl traverse × 1
- Rest 2 min, repeat 4 rounds

Log accuracy, quality, and coordination across all 4 rounds.

## Week 11 — Quadrupedal Flow

> **Flow** means transitioning between movement patterns without pause or loss of momentum. This week trains the connections between movements, not just the movements themselves.

### Session 1 — Quadrupedal Patterns

**Warm-up:** 5 min — bear crawl ×30s, gecko crawl ×30s, explosive bear crawl ×10s

---

#### Crab Walk
**Sets · Reps · Rest:** 4 × traverse and return · 60s rest
**Log type:** Quality (1–5)

> **Why:** Trains shoulder external rotation, hip extension, and posterior chain body tension in a reverse-facing quadrupedal pattern — the movement pattern used in underbar transitions and reverse traversal along low obstacles.

**How:** Sit on the floor. Place hands behind you, fingers pointing toward your feet (or slightly to the side for wrist comfort). Lift hips off the floor so body forms a table shape. Walk forward using opposite hand-foot coordination. Keep hips high.

**Cues:**
- Hips stay level with or above knee height — don't sag
- Fingers can point outward if wrist discomfort occurs
- Move deliberately — priority is form
- Gradually increase speed as pattern solidifies

---

#### Bear Crawl to Push-Up to Bear Crawl
**Sets · Reps · Rest:** 4 × 4 transitions · 90s rest
**Log type:** Quality (1–5)

> **Why:** Trains the transition between locomotion and a static position — the push transition in a vault where the body moves from running to arm-supported to running. The transition is always the hardest part.

**How:** Bear crawl 3 steps. Stop and perform 1 push-up from the bear crawl position. Return to bear crawl. 3 steps. Push-up. That is 1 rep. The transition must be smooth — no resetting, no pause.

**Cues:**
- The push-up position and bear crawl position are not far apart — practice the hip lowering/raising
- Keep core rigid through the transition
- Quality 5 = no pause, no visible adjustment at the transition point

---

#### Quadrupedal Flow Sequence
**Sets · Reps · Rest:** 4 × sequence × 2 · 2 min rest
**Log type:** Quality (1–5)

> **Why:** Sequences train movement linking — the ability to chain different patterns without thinking about transitions. This is the cognitive and physical foundation of flowing parkour movement.

**Sequence:**
1. Bear crawl forward 4 steps
2. Rotate to crab walk, travel 2 steps
3. Rotate back to bear crawl, 4 steps back
4. Stop, drop to plank, hold 3s
5. Back to bear crawl

Practice the sequence slowly until smooth, then gradually increase speed.

**Cues:**
- The rotations between patterns are the skill — practise them in isolation first
- Slow is smooth, smooth is fast — do not rush before the pattern is automatic
- Quality 5 = sequence flows as one continuous movement

**Cool-down:** 8 min — thoracic rotation stretch, child's pose, hip flexor series, wrist decompression

---

### Session 2 — Ground Movement

**Warm-up:** Full quadrupedal sequence from Session 1

---

#### Forward Roll (on mat or carpet)
**Sets · Reps · Rest:** 5 × 4 reps · 90s rest
**Log type:** Quality (1–5)

> **Why:** The forward roll is the fundamental fall recovery skill of parkour — converting uncontrolled downward momentum into forward momentum, avoiding impact trauma. On a mat or carpet, the risk is minimal. The skill is non-negotiable.

**How:** From a squat, tuck chin to chest. Place hands on the mat slightly in front. Roll diagonally across the upper back (NOT the spine directly) — right hand, right shoulder, diagonally across to left hip. Roll through and return to a squat. Begin very slowly. Do not rush.

**Cues:**
- The chin tuck is essential — it protects the head and spine
- Diagonal across the back — never directly down the spine
- Practice on soft surface only at this stage
- Quality 5 = smooth, quiet roll, stand up without hands

*Search: "parkour forward roll tutorial" for video reference.*

---

#### Underbar Movement Simulation (door frame belt)
**Sets · Reps · Rest:** 4 × 5 passes · 60s rest
**Log type:** Quality (1–5)

> **Why:** The underbar — passing through a gap below an obstacle — requires coordinated hip flexion, body tension, and spatial awareness in a constrained movement path. A belt or rope tied across the door frame creates a height-adjustable barrier requiring no additional equipment.

**How:** Tie a belt or length of rope across the door frame at hip height (loop around each side of the frame). Approach from a walk, duck under the belt pulling your hips through the gap first. The belt height should require you to actively tuck — not simply duck your head. Adjust height by repositioning the belt. Lower = harder.

**Cues:**
- Approach with momentum — not a slow duck
- Hips lead through the gap — tuck them in as you pass
- Head goes through last — chin tuck
- Increase approach speed as spatial confidence builds
- Lower the belt each week to progressively demand more hip flexion

### Session 3 — Spatial Awareness

**Warm-up:** 5 min — full joint circuit + quadrupedal flow sequence

---

#### Blind Reach and Placement
**Sets · Reps · Rest:** 3 × 10 placements each hand · 30s rest
**Log type:** Accuracy (cm off target)

> **Why:** In parkour, hand and foot placements are made at speed with visual attention on the route ahead — not on the surface being gripped. This trains proprioceptive placement accuracy without visual feedback.

**How:** Place a tape mark on the floor. Close your eyes. Reach down and place one hand on the mark. Open eyes and measure how far off you were. Repeat from different distances and angles.

**Cues:**
- Eyes closed before you start moving — not mid-reach
- Note whether you consistently miss in one direction — that is a proprioceptive bias to correct
- Progress: reduce miss distance across the session

---

#### Precision Jump with Distracted Vision
**Sets · Reps · Rest:** 4 × 4 jumps · 90s rest
**Log type:** Accuracy (cm) + stick quality

> **Why:** In practice, your eyes will be on the next obstacle, not the landing surface. This drill trains landing precision while visual attention is directed elsewhere.

**How:** Set tape targets as in Week 10. Before jumping, fix your gaze on a specific point on the wall above the target — not at the target itself. Jump and land on the mark using peripheral vision and spatial memory only. Stick. Measure accuracy.

**Cues:**
- Maintain the wall gaze point — do not let eyes drop to the target
- Spatial awareness develops with practice — early accuracy will be poor. Log and track improvement.
- Quality and accuracy should both be logged — they may trade off initially

**Cool-down:** 10 min — extended hip series, forward fold, wrist decompression, thoracic rotation
